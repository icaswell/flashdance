To deliberately distort facts or misrepresent the truth in order to mislead or deceive others.
Chengyu Story
This idiom originates from a story during the Warring States period in ancient China. A corrupt court official named Zhao Gao tried to deceive the Qin emperor by insisting that a deer was actually a horse. This illustrates how Zhao Gao blatantly distorted the facts to serve his own agenda and maintain his power.

This is from:                                      “Strategies of the Warring States – Yan Ce II” by Liu Xiang, Western Han Dynasty                                                       西汉·刘向《战国策·燕策二》).
在商业竞争中，我们应该避免陷入“鹬蚌相争，渔翁得利”的局面，以免让竞争对手有机可乘。
In business competition, we should avoid falling into the trap of “when the clam and the egret quarrel, the fisher benefits”, lest we give our competitors an opportunity to gain advantage.

青出于蓝而胜于蓝
qīng chū yú lán ér shèng yú lán
The student surpasses the master
Chengyu Story
The expression “the green is superior to the blue” comes from the Confucian master Xunzi’s “Advice on Learning”, which is a common saying that a person can be improved after learning or education. It is often used as a metaphor for a student surpassing his teacher or a descendant surpassing his predecessor. Xunzi used this to express the idea that students can surpass their teachers and successors can outshine their predecessors; the refined color indigo comes from blue but is deeper in hue.
“青出于蓝而胜于蓝”，我们期待年轻一代能在各个领域展现出更加出色的能力。
“Blue transformed becomes indigo,” we anticipate that the younger generation will demonstrate superior capabilities in various fields.

integrity and literary talent
Chengyu Story
Spring and Autumn is an idiom that first came from Western Jin Dynasty-Chen Shou’s “Three Kingdoms Zhi-Wei Zhi-Biography of Xing Stern. It refers to the blossoming of flowers in the spring and the bearing of fruit in the fall. It is a metaphor for literacy and virtue, and also extends to profound knowledge, high character and pure behavior, as well as the cause and effect relationship of things. 高山流水
gāo shān liú shuǐ
It is too hard that meet someone really knows u
Chengyu Story
“High Mountains and Flowing Water” belongs to one of the ten most ancient Chinese songs. Legend has it that the pre-Qin qin master Bo Ya once played the qin in a deserted mountain wilderness, and the woodcutter Zhong Ziqi was able to comprehend that this was a depiction of “E’e Ruoxi Taishan” and “Yang Yang Ruoxi Jianghe”. Bo Ya was shocked and said, “Good, the heart of the son is the same as my heart.” After Zhong Ziqi’s death, Bo Ya lost his soulmate, dropped his zither and stopped playing for the rest of his life, hence the song “High Mountains and Flowing Water”. Later, the idiom of “High Mountains and Flowing Water” evolved into a metaphor for a close friend or soulmate, as well as for a beautiful piece of music.
这首交响乐是真正的杰作，是 “高山流水 ”的完美典范，打动了所有听众的心。
The symphony was a true masterpiece, a perfect example of “high mountains and flowing water” that touched the hearts of all who listened.

柳暗花明
liǔ àn huā míng
good things are coming, the bad things are gone, and your wishes will come ture soon
Chengyu Story
The original meaning of the idiom “Liu Di Hua Ming” is to describe the spring scene of willow trees and blossoms, and to compare it with the situation of suddenly finding a way out of a difficult situation after many twists and turns. It is used to describe a sudden change from adversity to a positive situation. This is an idiom derived from the poetry of the literati, and the idiom is first found in Tang dynasty Wang Wei’s poem “The Morning Morning Morning. The original line describes the sudden appearance of a village after passing through a dark grove of willows and a field of bright flowers, illustrating a moment of surprise and delight.
经过几个月的奋斗，公司终于获得了一份大合同，证明了 “柳暗花明又一村”。
After months of struggle, the company finally secured a major contract, proving that “after the willows grow dark, the flowers become bright.”
